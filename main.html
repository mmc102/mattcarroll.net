9.2.2023 The Opal

Spray

There are a few quintessential photos of the crux pitch of The Opal on the internet â€” wispy white lines propagating across a panel of dark, lichen-covered granite. Over the years, I've seen these photos and always thought it would be a cool route to try.

Last year in Squamish, I had hoped to get on the route with Luke, who was working on rebolting it. Ultimately, I had a relatively heinous trip, working way too much and stress-climbing at night.

Fast forward to this year; I had plenty of time to try the route myself. I had a rather pathetic day attempting to get to the top of the wall and drop a rope. I got pitted riding a moss wave off a slab, thought I found the top, had the pleasure of hauling my rope out, and jugging 60m of vertical trees when I realized I was not on the route. I lazily coiled the rope and then immensely regretted it when the rope got tangled trying to rappel into what was actually The Opal.

I spent the next three days top-rope soloing the route. As part of my hardness training, I didn't take any rest days and didn't tape my fingertips. This predictability yielded a nice split, which was essential to my plan of getting two rest days.

I considered asking a few people to support me, but ultimately decided that the pressure of getting supported was a bit too high. I realized I could try to lead rope solo the route and figured I would give that a go before involving someone else.

(Brent has a great blog post about his lead rope solo setup, and I literally just copied and pasted his method.)

I started around 4 PM after work and hiked to the base. There was a massive queue of people at Rock On, which was a bit of a bummer. Faffing in the public eye is markedly worse than faffing in solitude.

Luke aced a nice sandbag on pitch one, claiming it was a "30m" pitch on Mountain Project. I dutifully tied my rope off at 40m, which fell short of the anchor, but luckily there was an anchor off to the right where I could stop.

After some ado, I began the first "hard" pitch. I was nervous because the whole rope system was still pretty new to me, and it has gear that's tenuous to place and almost impossible to evaluate. Perfectly, a companion from Rock On honed in on this moment to ask some critical questions:

"Hey, RopeSolo guy, is that Merci Me?"

"Hey, RopeSolo guy, what route is that?"

"Hey, RopeSolo guy with no shirt on"...

Luckily, he gave up on me when he managed to turn on his Bluetooth speaker and play "Like A Virgin" for everyone in the South Gully. In yet another stroke of luck, he didn't subject us to Madonna's drab voice but instead played his incredibly tasteful rendition.

Aside from that, the pitch went fine, and I completed the down-climb next without any issues.

The crux pitch also went extremely well. There is potential for a bad-ish ledge fall on the crux moves, and my strategy for this was to leave a decent chunk of rope out in hopes of being able to jump over the ledge. I had traded my warm-up shoes that I was using for rope soloing (new Boostics â€” think Ford Focus) for the Ferraris of climbing footwear â€” a mint pair of original Boostics. Normally, they make a pitch of that style at least one number grade easier, and I was shook by how massive the feet felt underfoot. I had to churn the butter a bit at the last boulder on the pitch to grab the jug, but ultimately it all felt nice and controlled.

The final two pitches went off without any real charades. At this point, I felt dialed in on the rope solo setup, but my elbows were hurting from all the jugging with a backpack. Fortunately, they are more or less entirely slab climbing, so my arms were of little service to the effort anyway.

I uneventfully topped out and laughed to myself, trying to decide if this was just another day of climbing or if I had just done something that would be notable in my climbing career. In hindsight, I think that was a perfect process for a route like that, and I'm glad it happened. It was notable enough for me to want to write about it so that I can reference the day without strictly relying on my brain-laundered memories, but really, nobody should care.

The Emotional Stuff

In the past, I've used soloing as a way to "rebuild" my relationship with myself. Forcing self-belief by exposing myself to really high stakes felt unhealthy but generally effective. This experience felt motivated by a similar desire to get reacquainted with myself, but I think this was healthier. I hope this signals some kind of emotional growth.

I've allowed climbing to obliterate so many things I have loved, yet it provides me with unmatched joy and fervor for life that I will continue to be unwilling to change my relationship with it.

8.18.2023 Squamish Updates

Overview

For my setup to work, I generally optimize for:

  • Reliable internet (averaging 1-2 Zoom calls per day)
  • Good access to climbing
  • A reasonable place to park the van at night
  • Weather
  • Community

I have Starlink, which has been working well for internet, but it still feels a bit unreliable for days with important meetings. It seems like most calls I make have momentary periods of instability, especially during times with many other people and screen sharing.
